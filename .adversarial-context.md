# Adversarial Review: Over-Articulated Dialogue and Scene Architecture in LLM Fiction Pipeline

## What we built
A visual novel story generation pipeline (v2) that produces full stories:
1. Seed/intake (user describes concept)
2. Bible generation: world, characters (with WANT/MISBELIEF/BREAK_POINT), plot (tension chain with "but"/"therefore" linking)
3. Scene planning: clusters beats into 6-12 scenes with pacing types (pressure_cooker, slow_burn, whiplash, aftermath, set_piece)
4. Scene generation: sequential, with cumulative tension state tracking between scenes
5. Postproduction: 5 editor passes + emotion mapper + VNBuilder packager

The scene writer already has extensive rules:
- TRANSMUTATION: background pressure guides writing implicitly via objects/gestures/silences
- INSTABILITY: scenes must have friction, failed intentions, non-optimal responses, power shifts
- WRONG-TURN RULE: misinterpretations, overreactions, dead-end questions
- Anti-slop, anti-literary guidance, VN register rules
- Content policy: full support for adult/erotic content

The tension tracking system (TensionState) already tracks:
- Relationship trajectories, unresolved threads, emotional temperature (1-10)
- what_the_reader_knows, what_hasnt_broken_yet (pressure available to release)
- Register history (to prevent tonal monotony)

Scenes failing vitality checks get a second roll.

## The actual problem (validated by user + external review)

We generated a 10-scene sci-fi story (compliance-tagged crew asset in a D/s dynamic aboard a military ship). External review scored it 8.3/10. User read it and agreed with key criticisms. The specific problems:

### Problem 1: Over-articulated dialogue (BIGGEST ISSUE)
Characters understand the story they're in. They deliver clean thematic statements instead of fumbling toward meaning like real humans.

Examples from the output:
- "I just need to know if I was there or if something that reads like me was there" — thesis-statement-level philosophical clarity while kneeling on a cold floor
- "Say something that isn't a variable" — a clever line that sounds WRITTEN, not spoken
- "I want you to answer a question without telling me what you did instead" — too meta, too articulate about conversational dynamics in the moment
- "You knew what my body was doing every time I was near you. Before I knew. For twenty-two cycles." — too articulate for someone in distress

The fundamental issue: the LLM projects its own articulateness onto characters. It generates dialogue that is coherent and meaningful. Human speech under pressure is neither.

The scene writer prompt has extensive rules about friction, instability, and transmutation — but NO rules about how characters speak vs. how the narrator speaks vs. how internal monologue works. The articulation hierarchy is missing entirely.

### Problem 2: Repetitive scene shapes
Despite having 5 pacing types available, scenes default to "controlled interaction -> internal disturbance -> quiet exit." Scenes 1, 2, and 3 all follow this pattern.

### Problem 3: Internal monologue overload
4-5 consecutive INTERNAL lines without action or dialogue. The writer fills silence with cognition instead of physical action or environment.

### Problem 4: Motif/phrase repetition across scenes
"There it is" appears in nearly every scene as internal monologue. "Something in my sternum" appears 5+ times. No cross-scene tracking of reused phrases.

### Problem 5: Emotion mapper defaults to "neutral"
Scene 9: character presses his mouth to another's wrist — emotion tagged "neutral." The mapper (Haiku) defaults to neutral for anything ambiguous.

### Problem 6: Speaker attribution bug
In scene 2, all of Dris's (the auditor's) spoken lines are attributed to "Commander Idris Solano" — the wrong character entirely.

## Why this matters for the pipeline
The scene writer prompt already has rules for dramatic friction. But over-articulated dialogue is a DIFFERENT failure mode — it's not about structure or plot, it's about the linguistic register of speech. A scene can have perfect instability, tension, and stakes, and still have dialogue that sounds fake because every character is too articulate.

## Proposed solutions (6 approaches under consideration)

### Approach 1: Narrative Voice Hierarchy (scene writer prompt change)
Add explicit articulation rules:
- NARRATION may understand and articulate the scene's meaning
- INTERNAL may approach meaning but never fully arrive — thoughts fragment, circle, contradict
- DIALOGUE must never articulate theme — characters talk about concrete things and the meaning emerges from gaps
- The reader should be AHEAD of the characters

### Approach 2: Character Voice Cards (bible generation addition)
During character generation, produce speech constraints per character:
- Max sentence length, tendency to complete or fragment thoughts
- What the character would NEVER articulate directly
- How they deflect when pushed, their specific verbal tics
- Fed to scene writer as hard constraints

### Approach 3: De-articulation pass (second call to same model after scene generation)
After writing, a second call reviews each dialogue line asking:
- Could this character realistically produce this sentence in this emotional state?
- Does this line articulate something the character shouldn't have access to?
- Would silence or a simpler statement be more honest?
Rewrites offending lines. Preserves genuine moments of clarity.

### Approach 4: Specialist dialogue agent (separate from scene writer)
Instead of one model writing everything, split the work:
- Scene writer produces narration, blocking, internal monologue, and dialogue PLACEHOLDERS
- Dialogue specialist writes only the spoken lines, given the physical scene as context
- Could focus entirely on voice accuracy and naturalism

### Approach 5: Physical-first generation (creative experiment)
Write the scene as pure physical action first (no dialogue), then add speech only where silence would be conspicuous. Inverts the LLM's default priority.

### Approach 6: "Wrong Answer" technique (creative experiment)
For key dialogue moments: generate what the character WOULD say with perfect self-knowledge, then deliberately degrade it — remove parts they wouldn't access, break syntax, replace articulate words with simpler ones.

### Supporting changes (lower risk, likely needed regardless):
- Mechanical constraints: max 2 consecutive INTERNAL lines, dialogue length guidance
- Cross-scene repetition tracker
- Emotion mapper fix (better compound emotions, no neutral default for ambiguous moments)
- State extraction between scenes (structured handoff of what changed)
- Scene shape enforcement at planning level

## What we want reviewed

Review broadly — don't limit to these concerns:

1. **Is the over-articulation diagnosis correct?** Is "characters understand the story they're in" the real problem, or is it something else? Could the existing instability/transmutation rules fix this if tuned differently?

2. **Which approach (1-6) would work best?** Are any redundant? Would any damage the writing? What's the right combination?

3. **Is a specialist dialogue agent (Approach 4) better than a de-articulation pass (Approach 3)?** What are the tradeoffs? The split risks coordination problems but could produce more natural speech. The pass risks being too timid or too aggressive.

4. **Would the voice hierarchy (Approach 1) be sufficient on its own?** Or is the LLM's over-articulation tendency too strong for prompt-level fixes alone?

5. **What's missing?** Are there problems or solutions we haven't identified? Is there a simpler fix nobody has proposed?

6. **How should we test these approaches?** We need a practical framework for comparing the output of different techniques on the same scenes. What would a good A/B test look like?

7. **What are the risks?** Which approaches could make the writing WORSE? What should we watch for?

## Important context
- The existing pipeline already has instability rules, tension tracking, vitality scoring. The problem is NOT missing dramatic infrastructure — it's missing speech register rules.
- The user specifically noted the dialogue "felt fake" — like it wasn't how people talk. This is the primary user concern.
- The pipeline already works well for plot, structure, world, and character. We don't want to damage those strengths while fixing dialogue.
- This is a solo developer project. Complexity has real cost. Simpler solutions preferred if equally effective.
